To write an AI compliance framework for Mauritius and the Southern Africa region, several
region-specific considerations and references are useful. Mauritius is making strong moves
toward AI adoption, reflected in regulatory sandboxes, the establishment of an AI Unit, and
comprehensive guidelines on use, ethics, and governance. Southern Africa as a whole is
pursuing responsible AI through both national and regional agreements, though few countries
have dedicated AI laws—most rely on existing data protection and sectoral regulation. [1] [2] [3]

Mauritius AI Compliance Foundations
Mauritius emphasizes:
    Regulatory sandboxes for AI pilots in finance and legal tech, balancing innovation with
    controlled risk.
    A Data Protection Act modeled on GDPR, ensuring strong data governance and privacy.
    Sector-specific rules—e.g., the Financial Services Commission's operational rules for AI-
    enabled services, and university guidelines for ethical AI.
    Government and public sector measures: the AI Unit in the Ministry of ICT, a Rs 25 million
    budget for AI integration, and education-linked guidelines for staff competency and ethical
    standards. [4] [5] [1]
Key compliance actions for Mauritius:
    Formal governance: Document models, use audit trails, and enforce human-in-the-loop
    verification.
    Adherence to international standards: ISO/IEC 42001 for AI governance, especially for
    legal/regulated sectors.
    Pilot projects in compliance-driven settings: Start with supervised use cases like contract
    review, regulatory filings, or public service automation—areas with clear legal frameworks
    and compliance requirements. [5] [4]
    Training and accountability: Ongoing staff training, clear lines of responsibility, and model
    inventories for full traceability.
Southern Africa Regional Compliance Context
Across Southern Africa, there are:
   The SADC Model Law on Data Protection, relevant for AI because it governs automated
   processing of personal data.
   The Windhoek Statement (2022), where Southern African governments committed to
   principles like capacity-building, investment, ethical and gender-inclusive AI, and cross-
   border partnership. [2]
   National approaches: South Africa’s draft AI policy emphasizes human-centered ethics,
   talent development, and alignment with existing privacy laws (e.g., POPIA). Most countries
   (e.g., Zambia, Zimbabwe) regulate AI indirectly through data protection and sectoral laws
   rather than dedicated AI statutes. [3] [6] [7]
Compliance best-practices in this region include:
   AI-by-design: Embed compliance and ethical review into system design and procurement.
   Alignment with African Union (AU) and UNESCO guidance: Prioritize fairness,
   transparency, and inclusivity at each deployment stage.
   Regional infrastructure and standards: Design frameworks considering cross-border data
   flows and compatibility with continental directives. [8] [9] [3]
   Continuous engagement: Collaborate with local regulators and stakeholders using
   sandboxes and public consultations.

Key Compliance Checklist
   Confirm all AI systems are GDPR-compliant or locally equivalent (Mauritius DPA, SADC
   Model Law).
   Conduct regular risk and impact assessments before deployment.
   Maintain model registries and audit logs for all AI uses.
   Ensure human oversight in high-risk decisions.
   Embed ethics review and public reporting for transparency.

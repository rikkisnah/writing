## üìö Theme 1: The Books That Sharpened My Thinking

I read dozens of books this year. These five stayed with me.

**Confronting Evil: Assessing the Worst of the Worst** reminded me that evil is real, recurring, and must be named plainly to be confronted. In a year where diplomatic language often obscured atrocity, this book was a cold glass of water to the face.

**Range: Why Generalists Triumph in a Specialized World** validated something I've long believed‚Äîthat breadth beats narrowness. In a world obsessed with "10,000 hours" specialization, David Epstein makes the case that adaptive thinkers win in complex, changing environments. As someone who moved from private equity to infrastructure engineering to opinion writing, this felt personal.

**The Good Immigrant** told stories of immigration, identity, and belonging with honesty, pain, and humanity. It forced me to reckon with my own immigrant experience‚Äîleaving Mauritius, building a career in Seattle, and the strange liminal space of belonging to two places and neither.

**Working For A Narcissist** was practical medicine. Toxic leadership is endemic in high-performance environments. This book offers survival tactics for power-imbalanced situations without pretending the problem is simple.

**God, AI and the End of History** provided the theological lens I didn't know I needed. While Silicon Valley debates alignment, this book asks deeper questions about meaning, purpose, and the limits of machines. It's not anti-AI; it's anti-hubris.

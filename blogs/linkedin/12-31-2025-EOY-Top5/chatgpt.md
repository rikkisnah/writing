## **2025: Two Worlds, Zero Illusions, Two Forces, Five Signals**

Every year has noise.
A few years have signal.

2025 is one of those years where the signal is impossible to ignore. It is not subtle. It is not abstract. It is physical, political, theological, economic, and deeply human.

I’ve been reflecting on 2025 through a simple lens—**2-0-2-5**—not as numerology, but as structure.

Two worlds.
Zero illusions.
Two dominant forces.
Five signals that matter.

This is not a “best of” list. It’s a **map of where pressure is building**—and where it will break next.

## **2 — Two Worlds Are Now Fully Visible**

We now live in **two parallel worlds**, and pretending otherwise is the fastest way to fall behind.

The first is the **virtual world**—models, tokens, abstractions, narratives, feeds. It moves fast, talks loud, and optimizes for attention.

The second is the **physical world**—power plants, data centers, silicon, supply chains, cooling, land, labor, energy, and geopolitics.

In 2025, the illusion that software alone matters finally collapsed.

AI is no longer a cloud metaphor. It is concrete, steel, uranium, fiber, and sovereign policy. Compute has mass. Latency has geography. Energy has politics.

This year made it obvious: **the winners of the next decade will be those who can bridge both worlds without confusing one for the other.**

## **0 — Zero Illusions Left**

If there’s a single theme that cuts across everything I read, built, and observed in 2025, it’s this:

**Illusions are expensive.**

Illusions about leadership.
Illusions about institutions.
Illusions about morality.
Illusions about AI itself.

The books that stayed with me this year all shared a refusal to soften reality.

* **Confronting Evil** forced an uncomfortable reminder: evil is not theoretical, rare, or solved. It repeats when unnamed.
* **Range** quietly dismantled the cult of narrow specialization, showing why breadth beats optimization in chaotic systems.
* **The Good Immigrant** rejected sanitized narratives of belonging and told the truth—messy, painful, human.
* **Working for a Narcissist** stripped power dynamics down to mechanics, not morality.
* **God, AI and the End of History** reminded me that intelligence is not meaning, and capability is not wisdom.

Across all of them, the message was consistent: **clarity is kinder than comfort**.

## **2 — Two Forces Now Shape the World**

By mid-2025, it became clear that nearly every global event sits downstream of **two dominant forces**.

### **Force #1: Geopolitics Has Re-Entered Engineering**

The return of hard power politics is no longer subtle.

The election of **Donald Trump** alongside **J.D. Vance** marked a full acceleration of America-First economics: tariffs, trade pressure, immigration controls, and explicit industrial policy.

AI infrastructure is now treated like oil fields or interstate highways.

The **Stargate-scale AI initiatives** dwarf historical scientific programs—not because of curiosity, but because of leverage. Compute is power. Energy is strategy. Talent is national security.

Europe debates regulation.
China scales alternatives.
The U.S. builds.

The gap is no longer theoretical.

### **Force #2: AI Has Escaped the Lab**

2025 was the year AI stopped being a “tool” and became **infrastructure**.

* Massive capital flowed into compute.
* Reasoning models and agentic systems crossed key thresholds.
* Hardware timelines—not algorithms—became the constraint.

Stories like China’s **DeepSeek R1** weren’t shocking because of capability, but because they signaled **multipolar AI reality**.

This is no longer a winner-takes-all model race. It’s a **who controls the factories** race.

## **5 — Five Signals That Actually Matter**

Everything above collapses into five concrete signals I’m carrying forward.

### **1. Books Are Still the Highest-Bandwidth Signal**

Short content reacts.
Books reveal structure.

My top five this year weren’t about trends. They were about **how humans behave under pressure**—power, fear, ambition, identity, and belief. That matters more than any framework.

### **2. Writing Is Becoming a Historical Act**

The most important article I read this year was the *New York Times* piece on AI and historical scholarship. It made one thing clear:

> **Who writes history now includes machines.**

That raises uncomfortable questions about truth, curation, bias, and permanence. Once AI enters the archive, **history itself becomes a systems problem**.

This is why I continue to write—technical deep dives like my NCCL guide, infrastructure pieces at OCI, and cultural essays for Mauritius. Writing is no longer commentary. It is preservation.

### **3. Infrastructure Is the New Differentiator**

In 2025, AI leadership shifted decisively from models to **who can operationalize hardware at scale**.

Projects like NVIDIA GB200 NVL72 inside **Oracle** are not glamorous. They are hard, slow, unforgiving engineering.

That’s where the moat is.

This is also why Oracle’s stock volatility misses the point. Markets debate CapEx. Builders understand compounding infrastructure advantage.

### **4. Energy Will Replace Chips as the Bottleneck**

AI doesn’t just consume electricity. It **reshapes grids**.

2026 will be remembered as the year nuclear energy stopped being ideological and became inevitable. SMRs, restarts, private power agreements—“feeding the beast” is now the primary constraint.

The limiting reagent of intelligence is no longer silicon. It’s watts.

### **5. Physical AI Will Humble Us**

Robotics will have its “uncanny valley” moment.

Humanoid demos will go viral—for the wrong reasons. Failures will be public. Expectations will reset.

That’s healthy.

Progress will come not from general-purpose robots, but from **specialized, constrained, teleoperated systems** that respect physics instead of fighting it.

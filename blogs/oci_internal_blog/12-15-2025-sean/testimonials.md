## From Donald Lu

As you might have heard, Sean has decided to take a break. His last day in Oracle will be 12/19.

I'm very happy for Sean and want to take the moment to thank Sean for what he has done for OCI. Without Sean, Compute would not have been where we are today; actually, far from where we are today. If I put Sean's contribution together, it's a good reflection of key challenges Compute has gone through and the reasons for why we have some success today.

### Part of the Founding Members

When Sean and I joined OCI (called Bare Metal cloud) at that time, Compute was about 25 people and we all know each other. Sean's knowledge and the value he holds played a big role in Compute, and helped shape the culture of nailing the basics, act-now, iterate, don't be a jerk, and many other aspects.

### NPI and Ingestion

X7 was a big deal for IAD (which was a new region under build at that time) and we couldn't get that machine to run stable (sounds familiar, right?). Sean and Thomas hand ingested many machines, solved T73 training issues and eventually were able to get the HW running. Sean also helped grow a true NPI team and passed on the torch.

### Spectre / Meltdown

That was the first test of OCI (still called Bare Metal Cloud at that time) as a proper cloud provider. Even though he wasn't part of the VM team at the time, he played a big role in understanding the vulnerability, planning and executing the mitigation.

### L1TF and Many Others

As side channel attacks continue to grow, Sean took over the key role of security analysis and leading mitigation execution from Compute's perspective. He worked with OL and security team, led many responses to various side channel attack events.

### Broadcom Drama

We got a real taste of the liability of running a VM platform in the Broadcom drama. We had tens to hundreds of VM reboots every day (keep in mind we had very small fleet at that time), and the situation lasted for weeks. Even Broadcom didn't know what was going on. Eventually, they sent people to Seattle, projected their source code for us (Sean) to inspect. I was in the room, when Sean pointed out the bug in their code that can cause the issue; and before he saw the code he already knew the bug through reverse engineering. Sean developed a fix to live patch the fleet using some kind of debugging to change the instruction to the correct width and fixed the issue. This is one of those that sounds like a legend.

Sean also drove the effort to get off Broadcom for all future shapes and introduced Mellanox to OCI.

### Sub-core VM

Sean prototyped the algorithm which eventually got into our sub-core VM implementation.

### GPUs, Before It Was a Thing

Before GPU was a real business, which was P100 and V100 timeline, Sean led the GPU NPI; we even made GPU VM work at that time, including the ability to segment NVLink domains. There was a demo he did in some kind of all-hands (Compute or OCI, I forgot) about fluid simulation that I still remember vividly.

### Zoom

Zoom was a very big deal in OCI at one time. Before we had any infra-heavy customers, they were running tens of thousands of VMs and was the single, biggest external paying customer. Sean helped troubleshoot several hard problems, from their own security agent consuming too much CPU, to some networking issues on host and OCI networking side, to some very interesting ones like CPU got throttled to 1 GHz. That was the first time we got a taste of how to serve some large, external customer.

### PCIe-bifurcation

Sean's knowledge and drive made it happen. Now it is a standard in our hardware, used by all hypervisors and BMs on all large customers.

### Telesis

Telesis was, and still is a very demanding customer, and they really know what they are doing. It was not easy to win their trust in early days, and Sean played a big role. SR-IOV setup for their VMs, PCIe bifurcation configuration, Debian image readiness, various Mellanox firmware issues, etc. We solved many hard problems to get us here.

### Container Instance

Sean was one main designer of Container Instance; we explored multiple options like Kata container and eventually landed in QEMU microVM solution.

### GB200 Bring-up

Sean was in AGA, SYD for first couple of GB200 QS and PS bring-up. We have thousands of racks running in many data centers today, bringing in billion $ level ACR.

### GB200 Manufacturing

Sean helped solve some hard provisioning problems, established how yield should be measured; the triage service he built is still running today in Houston.

### GPU Ingestion

Sean helped stabilize HSG network; his drive helped us know more about IB (PLR, etc) and his insights helped us develop a new way to watch link health.

### Memory Over-subscription

Sean was part of the crew that invented the hypervisor working set size measurement, which is the foundation of dynamic-resource VM we are actively rolling out with the goal to save hundreds of millions $ in memory costs.

I will stop here so the email doesn't become too long and not readable. Thank you, Sean, for your contribution to the business, for your kindness to all the people you have worked with, and to your leadership that holds a lot of us together as a team. It has been a true privilege to have worked side by side with you over the last 9+ years.
